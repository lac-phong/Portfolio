This project not only taught me failure, but hope and the power of determination as well, as our group, despite having to restart, put even more effort into our final product, "Paindemine". In terms of maximum theorhetical speed, "Paindemine" was far from what was thought for "Tambibien". For "paindemine" we had completely abandoned the gear ratio and focused on a simple working car. But even then, "Paindemine" showed great promise, as it not only worked, but was also faster than a lot of the other competition. Working on this project, even with its tough situations and problems, was a blast and I had a great time communnicating and working with Darion, Keagan, and Khalil on this project. West Point Bridge

POE 7: March 7th 2019

The West Point Bridge project revolved around the use of Bridge Designer. This program allowed users to build bridges, using a variety of members, materials, etc. and simulate how well the bridge performs under real-life scenarios. The goal of this project was to design a working bridge on Bridge Designer that was less than or equal to the $300,000 budget and 12-24 meters above the water. The performance of the bridge was based on how much stress or tension individual members went through and how much the bridge bent in the simulation overall. My job in this project was to design my own 2 working bridges and compare them with Micheal's 2 designs. I also had to collaborate with Michael to work on the Descision Matrix, Bridge modifications, as well as the overall documentation of the project. For the documentation, I was responsible for the Design Brief, part of the research, and the Final Justification of the bridge. From this project, I learned the different steps it takes to make an efficient bridge and understand the loop holes in getting the most efficient bridge. For example, to make the bridges cheaper, using hollow tubes was the most effective way to maximize performance, however solid tubes were necessary in certain areas if the members were undergoing too much tension. Making the member's surface area bigger was important if the members were undergoing too much compression and the use of different materials was crucial in the dispersion of forces throughout the bridge. Overall, I not only took away a better understanding of bridges and the aspects of compression and tension, but also the wonders of collaboration and teamworking from the modifications and decision matrix I did with Michael. Interactive Seating Chart

APCSA 3: September 12th 2019

The Interactive Seating Chart was our intro Java project that Mr. Kaehms gave us. It utilized the IDE known as GreenFoot which allowed us to play around the concept of manipulating objects visually. Our task for this project was to create an object of ourselves given where we were seated in the class. Whoever using the program would be able to click on you, executing a brief animation that was unique and making your object change from sitting position to standing position, then ask you a few questions. Your object would respond properly and when asked to sit down it would return from standing position to sitting position. The concept of finishing your personal object was relatively easy because it required very simple java commands to change an object's image from one to another and make it spin around or run around the screen. However, the complexity of this project was the fact that at the very end, the entire class had to figure out a clean way to push to a single repository in GitHub without any merge conflicts. This was difficult because it relied on everyone knowing what they were doing. There were many cases in the process of trying to get the entire code working where a person would accidently merge it into master which messed up everything because we had to keep master clean until the very end (at least for how our build master wanted things to operate). At the very end we were able to get everything done on time and our classroom was fully functional by the end of this project. It got everyone to know each other and it opened me to how complex yet fun collaborative coding could be. Black Adam!

APCSA 3: October 12th 2019

The "Black Adam!" project utilized Android Studio in order to make an app for android devices. Our app is basically an app that serves as a collection of songs you can click on and look at lyrics. This allows the users to sing along with their friends and family to any song. The app consists of five activities that each served a different purpose. The login screen allows the user to input their credentials, the genre screen allows the user to choose which genre they want, the song screen allows the user to choose the songs they want to listen to, the lyric display allows the user to look at the lyrics for the song they chose, and the settings screen allows the user to change the app's configurations and visual asthetic to their own personal liking. The main part that made this project especially challenging was getting each activity to interact and change based on what the user chose. To do this I implemented an onClick method that would transport data from one activity to another so that the next activity would be able to adapt and change based on the information from the pervious activity. Overall, it was a great introduction to the IDE Android Studio and Java overall and made me realize the endless potential that java held because of it's nature as an object-oriented langauge. Rock Paper Scissors

DE 6: October 21st 2020

The Rock Paper Scissors project was the first project of the year for Digital Electronics. It was in this project that I was able to utilize all that we learned in the first unit (using Multisim, the wiring on a breadboard with ICs and jumper cables, understanding truth tables, inputs, and outputs). The project's goal was to make a circuit system that solved any problem. It only required the system to have at least 3 inputs and 1 output. However, I wanted to challenge myself. The problem or moreover the opportunity my rock paper scissors game is meant to offer is to help kids understand the basics of logic through a rock paper scissors game. I utilized 4 inputs and 3 outputs (Inputs: rock, paper, scissors, same | Outputs: rock win, paper win, scissor win, tie). Using the basic logic of how rock paper scissors works, I put together a multisim to base my circuit off of. To see what I did in more detail take a look at my documentation under "Visit Page". The primary takeaway from this project for me was understanding the entire process of circuiting. Starting out brainstorming the logical equations for the circuit, multisiming, and then finally putting it all together on the breadboard, it was truly an eye-opening and overall fun experience for me and it got me excited for the future in Digital Electronics.

Let's Go Seniors!

DE 6: March 5th 2021

This was the project I made for Unit 2.3 of my Digital Electronics class. The core concept behind the project was to make a fun message board that would make my fellow seniors smile. This is why I named the project "Let's Go Seniors". I first made a simulation of the project (on Multisim) to get a better image of how it would all work. This simulation would then be programmed into a PLD that would then execute the logic I created in the simulation. Through a 7-segment display and PLD, I programmed the message “Senior Year Pog Pog” into the PLD to display on the breadboard. The overall circuit displays one letter at a time and I utilized the internal clock of the PLD to make a 4-bit counter that iterates through all the inputs and outputs of the AOI logic in the correct order. The goal of this message is to rejuvenate the stressed seniors in high school with energy and get them to become motivated to finish off school and prepare themselves for the exciting experiences in college in the upcoming year. Overall, the project was really fun and it opened my eyes to the possibilities of a circuit board through the use of PLDs.
